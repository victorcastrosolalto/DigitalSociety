The Journey of Evolution

From room-sized giants to the cloud in your pocket. Explore the five generations that defined our digital world.

1940s – mid-1950s

1st Gen: Vacuum Tubes & Drums

The era of brute force. Machines were colossal, power-hungry, and filled entire rooms to perform calculations a smartphone handles in an instant.

Vacuum Tubes

Fragile glass tubes controlling electric flow. 10" Selectron tubes stored only 256–4096 bits.

Punched Cards

Stiff paper with hole patterns. Used in mechanical looms as early as 1725!

Brute Force Stats

  • Memory Type: Magnetic Drum
  • Typical Speed: 12,500 RPM
  • Capacity (IBM 650): ~10 Kilobytes
mid-1950s – mid-1960s

2nd Gen: Transistors & Random Access

The era where computers began to "remember" reliably. The shift from linear tape to random access memory (RAM) changed everything.

IBM RAMAC 305

The 1st Hard Drive. 50 spinning 24-inch discs holding 5MB.

Magnetic Core

Tiny magnetic rings woven with wire. Standard until the 70s.

Breakthrough:

"Previously, no one had thought of databases seriously, simply because there was no way that large amounts of data could be stored."

Jumpstarted the Database Field
mid-1960s – 1971

3rd Gen: The Integrated Circuit

Miniaturization begins. Instead of wiring parts, engineers "printed" them onto silicon chips.

The DRAM Revolution

Intel 1103 (1970)

A 1-kilobyte chip that marked the "beginning of the end for magnetic core memory."

1971 – Late 1990s

4th Gen: The Microprocessor

Democratization. One chip could now perform the functions that previously required an entire cabinet of electronics.

4k
Intel 4004 (1971)

2,300 transistors. The first general-purpose CPU on a single chip.

Portable Storage

Cassettes (660kB), Floppies, and the massive CD-ROM (1984).

Key Concept

Personal Computer Revolution

Miniaturization took the power of computing out of large corporations and put it into the hands of individuals. This era set the stage for a connected global network.

Late 1990s – Present

5th Gen: Cloud & Intelligence

Ambient & Ubiquitous Computing. No longer a box on a desk, computing exists in the cloud, our cars, and our pockets.

Cloud (2007)

Network-based storage like Dropbox.

AI & IoT

Decentralized data and intelligent systems.

IB DS Critical Perspectives

Physical to Invisible

The trend isn't just "smaller." It's the move from computers as distinct tools to computing as an invisible layer of society.

Global Connectivity

The shift from standalone processing to network-based storage (Cloud) is what allows for the current "seismic shifts" in society.

Legacy of Innovation

Each generation is built on the breakthroughs of the last. No generation is "obsolete"; they are the foundation of modern marvels.
