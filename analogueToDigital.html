Capturing the Analog World

Our world is a smooth gradient; computers are a set of blocks. Discover how the ADC acts as the bridge between reality and data.

Analog vs. Digital

Think of a Ramp versus a Staircase. One is continuous, the other is made of distinct, measurable steps.

Analog Signal

Continuous wave that varies smoothly. Infinite values possible. Found in sound, light, and pressure.

Digital Signal

Discrete values (0s and 1s). Robust against noise, easily copied, and perfectly processable by software.

Continuous
Discrete

Practicality Note

"Digital signals are far more robust... less prone to distortion."

The ADC Pipeline

The Analog-to-Digital Converter performs a rapid three-step translation to bridge the gap.

1

Sampling

Taking "snapshots" of the signal at regular intervals. Like frames in a movie, more samples equal a smoother representation.

Nyquist Theorem

Sample rate must be ≥ 2x the highest frequency.

Aliasing

Error when sampling rate is too low, creating false signals.

2

Quantization

Measuring the amplitude of each snapshot and rounding it to the nearest available numerical value.

Bit Depth

Number of levels on our "ruler." (e.g., 16-bit = 65,536 levels).

Quantization Error

The rounding difference; causes distortion/hiss in audio.

3

Encoding

Assigning a unique binary code (strings of 0s and 1s) to each measured level.

Real World Journey

Tracing a sound from a voice to a digital file and back.

1. Input (Transducer)

Microphone converts air vibrations into an analog electrical signal.

2. Translation (ADC)

The 3-step process converts electrical waves into a stream of binary data.

3. Processing & Storage

Data is stored as a file, processed by software, or transmitted globally.

4. Output (DAC)

The process reverses. A DAC reconstructs the wave for your speakers.
